Samuel Johnson, A Dictionary of the English Language (1755)Bug. Bugbear. A frightful object. a walking spectre, imagined to be seen. generally now used for a false terrour to frighten babees.Rawhead. The name of a spectre, mentioned to frighten children.Oxford English Dictionary (1971)Bugbear. A sort of hobgoblin (presumably in the shape of a bear) supposed to devour naughty children. hence, generally, any imaginary being invoked by nurses to frighten children.Webster's New International Dictionary of the English Language , 2nd edition (1947)Bugbear. A kind of imaginary goblin or specter, used to excite needless fear, as in children. now, any object of dread or abhorrence. Syn.   bugaboo, bogy.Webster's Third New International Dictionary of the English Language (1947)Boogeyman. Boogerman. Boogieman. A monstrous imaginary figure used in threatening children. Be good or the boogeyman will get you.Funk and Wagnalls Standard Dictionary of Folklore, Mythology, and Legend (1984)Bogey. Bogie. Bogy. A terrifying spirit of English folklore, of uncertain, but probably hobgoblinish nature. invoked especially to frighten children.
